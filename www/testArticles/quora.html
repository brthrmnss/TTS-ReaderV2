Britney Fitzgerald, lives in New York City
Answered 6h ago · Upvoted by Lia Connor, lives in New York City (1989-present) and Andrew Karam, lives in New York City (2009-present)

I love the buzz of the city. The energy is intoxicating—especially on a warm night in one of the Villages.

I love rooftop views. We’re so lucky to look at an epic skyline everyday.

I love the exposure to different cultures. My neighbors are from Turkey, Ohio, and Ukraine. All of these people bring an interesting story to the city.

I love going out to dinner. If you want the best <insert food here>, I’m confident (with some research) you will find it New York City. I’ve become very spoiled by my close proximity to amazing restaurants. Even some bodegas are world-class!

I love all the little side streets in the East and West Villages. It’s fun to roam these neighborhoods and stop in thrift stores or bookshops along the way. Then, come dinner time, you can find a cute little cafe for a glass of wine. (My favorites, here.)

I dislike crowed subway cars during the commute.

I dislike the amount of noise that can seep into my apartment.

I dislike February, when the holiday lights come down but it’s still so dark outside and the weather is unbearably cold.

I dislike when people clog up the sidewalks and escalators. Don’t they know we’re all in a hurry! ;)

Diane Chehab, lived in New York City
Answered Mon

The wonderful side of New York is the diversity, if you know to take advantage of it and don’t just relate to people of your own ethnicity. You can have a dinner party from people of all kinds of backgrounds, ethnic, national, even educational.

Explore the corners of Chinatown, it’s great fun. Try the food. If you don;t like something, don’t get it next time! I find the coffee better in Chinatown than in the business districts. And it’s all very inexpensive.

In the outer boroughs, such as Jackson Heights (Queens), there are enclaves of South Asians; in Flushing, an enormous Asian district. Japanese favor the Upper East Side in the fifties (i.e. 50th Street in Manhattan). Koreans midtown around 34th Street. Middle Easterners are in a couple of parts of Brooklyn nowadays. Russians near Coney Park. And so on.

You can go out to various cultural events, whether for free or not. There are gallery openings mostly on Thursdays (see the other reaches on the West Side in the twenties). Free concerts and movies on the summer.

I love walking over the Brooklyn Bridge, but you have to be wary of bicycles. It’s fun in the summer and even in the winter - just bundle up.

What I don’t like:

There are just too many people! So many crowds, that sometimes you can barely move on the sidewalk! The subway is always crowded: it’s good for safety reasons, but no fun when you can barely squeeze in a subway car at 9 pm. People can be aggressive, too, as they are squeezed and prodded, and maybe have their own personal problems (not enough money, a terrible apartment, etc.). Let’s not even mention rush hour!

The weather became very unpredictable. You used to know what to expect. Now, beyond usually counting on a frigid few days in January, you never quite know. May can be cool and April sunny. July can be stifling or not.

As for working there, if you are not a native New Yorker, you may not do as well as you deserve, if you don’t have the right connections. It’s usually a little easier when you were born and bred there.
